Mabo and the Rule of Native Title

Terra Nullius · Indirect Rule · International Legal Precedent

What is Terra Nullius?

Terra nullius — Latin for "land belonging to no one" — was the legal fiction that Australia, at the time of British settlement from 1788, was effectively unoccupied. Terra Nullius arguably originated from the Roman law concept of 'res nullius' (things owned by no one), but does not appear to have been used until the 18th/19th c. Under this doctrine, the common law arguably treated the continent as legally empty: without sovereign peoples, without law, without recognisable property rights. Nuance is needed here, though, as 'res nullius' and 'terra nullius' were sometimes employed negatively (e.g. to suggest that indigenous people did validly occupy and sometimes had sovereignty over their lands, even in the 19th century.) See Fitzmaurice's work for more on this.

The doctrine was not simply a misunderstanding. It was a foundational ideological instrument of settler colonialism, providing legal legitimacy for dispossession. If Indigenous Australians had no prior legal relationship to land, the Crown could acquire absolute sovereignty and underlying title without treaty, conquest, or compensation.

The rejection of terra nullius "removed the ideological foundation of settler colonisation" — but a different form of legitimation of settler occupation was needed and found.

— Patrick Wolfe, cited in Silverstein (2007)

Terra Nullius Before Mabo

In Australian legal history, the doctrine operated quietly and pervasively. Courts treated Aboriginal people as having no legal title to land, and colonial legislation proceeded on that assumption for nearly two centuries. The High Court in Mabo v Queensland (No 2) (1992) was the first to confront and overturn this position directly.

Legal Effect

Land was treated as Crown property from the moment of British settlement. Aboriginal people had no standing to assert prior ownership in colonial courts.

Historical Fiction

Australia was, of course, inhabited. The fiction was maintained not out of ignorance but out of the structural needs of settler colonialism.

International Context

Other jurisdictions — Canada, New Zealand — used conquest or treaty frameworks instead, which at least acknowledged prior Indigenous presence.

Comparison: Rhodesia

In In re Southern Rhodesia [1919], the Privy Council extinguished native title by finding the peoples were "too low in the scale of social organization." This argument was rejected in Mabo as contrary to modern human rights standards.

Mabo and the Overturning of Terra Nullius

The Mabo judgment, delivered by the High Court on 3 June 1992, formally rejected the doctrine of terra nullius and held that a form of native title survived British acquisition of sovereignty — provided it had not been extinguished by subsequent Crown grants or legislation.

The court's reasoning was cautious. Rather than addressing the validity of British sovereignty itself (declared non-justiciable), it confined its analysis to beneficial title. The sovereignty question — and the full implications of an inhabited colony — was deliberately left unresolved.

Behrendt's critique (2002): The overturning of terra nullius was "an important legal, symbolic and psychological turning point," but the practical benefits were quickly circumscribed — particularly by the Native Title Amendment Act 1998, passed without consultation with Indigenous peoples.

Critically, Silverstein argues that by replacing terra nullius with native title, the settler state simply substituted one form of legitimation for another. The new regime recognised a limited Indigenous presence, but only for those who could demonstrate unbroken continuity with pre-sovereignty laws and customs — a threshold that excluded the majority of Aboriginal Australians, particularly urban and southern communities.

The Aftermath: A New Legal Fiction?

Behrendt (2002) argues that Mabo created a legal fiction of "settlement" to replace terra nullius. While the fiction of an empty land was overturned, the High Court's refusal to adjudicate the validity of British sovereignty left the foundations of the Australian state legally unexamined.

Meanwhile, the political climate surrounding Mabo — especially the Howard government's "ten-point plan" in response to the Wik decision — demonstrated the vulnerability of court-won Indigenous rights to legislative override. Howard's rhetoric of "equal laws for all Australians" obscured the fact that property law had never been applied equally: terra nullius itself was the mechanism of dispossession.

While Australia has a dominant group who embraces a psychological terra nullius, any legal advances are vulnerable to legislative extinguishment.

— Larissa Behrendt, Mabo: Ten Years On (2002)

What is Indirect Rule?

Indirect rule was the dominant strategy of British colonial administration across Africa in the early twentieth century. Rather than governing colonised populations directly — an expensive and politically risky process — the British devolved local administrative power to indigenous chiefs and native councils, operating under British supervision.

Lord Lugard, Governor of Nigeria and architect of the system, formulated its philosophy in his 1921 treatise The Dual Mandate in British Tropical Africa: the colonising power had a dual obligation, to develop the empire's resources and to advance the subject peoples — with indirect rule presented as the mechanism best achieving both aims simultaneously.

Indirect administration is designed to adapt for the purposes of local government the tribal institutions which the Native peoples of Africa have evolved for themselves, so that the latter may develop in a constitutional manner from their own past.

— Donald Cameron, Governor of Tanganyika (1935), cited in Silverstein

The Three Pillars of Indirect Rule

Silverstein, drawing on Mahmood Mamdani's analysis, identifies three institutional foundations on which the system rested:

The Tribe

With fixed, "impervious and unchanging boundaries" — a colonial construction that simplified and often invented the social structures it purported to govern.

The Chief

A "despotic" authority whose power was endorsed, and in practice created, by the colonial regime. The chief was central to land allocation — his prestige depended on controlling land.

Customary Law

A codified body of "native law" that was, in reality, produced in the colonial encounter — shaped by British administrators, anthropologists, and indigenous elites.

The Bifurcated State

Mamdani's concept: a state split between a "civilised" urban sphere governed by modern law, and a "tribal" rural sphere governed by indirect rule and customary law.

Indirect Rule and Land

Land was central to the system. As Lugard wrote, the prestige of chiefs depended on their acknowledged right to allocate land. Native title — the idea that pre-colonial Indigenous communities had a recognised, if limited, interest in land — was the legal mechanism that gave this structure juridical legitimacy.

The codification of customary land law in native courts was essential to incorporation of indigenous societies into the colonial state. But as Martin Chanock showed, these "customs" were not simply recorded — they were created in the colonial encounter, shaped by the interests of Empire and serving as "instruments of colonial land policies."

Key tension: Indirect rule required maintaining the fiction of continuity — that customary law was timeless and authentic — while simultaneously transforming indigenous societies to serve colonial economic interests.

Silverstein's Argument: Mabo as Indirect Rule in Australia

Silverstein's central thesis is that the native title doctrine established in Mabo constitutes the formal introduction of indirect rule into Australian law. He traces a direct lineage from colonial Africa to the High Court of Australia.

This chart crudely compares British indirect rule in Africa with native title in Australia (legible to lawyers mainly in Privy Council decisions):

Feature Indirect Rule in Africa Native Title in Australia
Who qualifies "Tribal" peoples not "detribalised" by colonial contact Indigenous peoples maintaining continuous traditional laws and customs since sovereignty
Exclusion mechanism Urban/detribalised natives governed by separate law Urban and southern Aboriginal people effectively excluded
Basis of rights Continuity of tribal custom, not justice for dispossession Continuity of traditional laws, not restoration of what was stolen
Epistemology British courts adjudicate the content of "native custom" Australian courts determine what counts as traditional law and custom

The Yorta Yorta case (2002) illustrates the logic vividly: the Yorta Yorta people were found to have "ceased to exist" as a traditional society, their customs having been interrupted by colonial contact — thus losing any native title claim. In the language of 1920s colonial administration, they had been "detribalised."

The Privy Council and the Development of Native Title

Silverstein's historical argument rests on a crucial genealogy: the legal doctrines used in Mabo were developed not in Australia but in a series of Privy Council decisions arising from land disputes in British West Africa between 1915 and the 1930s. The High Court consciously relied on this jurisprudence in Mabo.

Understanding these cases is essential to understanding both the content and the limits of native title in Australia.

⸻
1915 — Foundational Narrative

Attorney-General of Southern Nigeria v John Holt: Lord Shaw characterised British colonisation of Lagos as a humanitarian intervention to suppress the slave trade. Within this narrative, there was no intention to abolish native property rights. The commercial character of land in Lagos (plots, piers, stores) was treated as evidence of native custom evolving into capitalist property forms.

1919 — Extinguishment Permitted

In re Southern Rhodesia: In a settler colony context, the Privy Council held that the Matabele and Mashona peoples had no recognisable property rights, characterising them as practising only "rude agriculture" and at a "low stage of social organization." Native title was extinguished by conquest. Lord Sumner presented this as an inevitable and beneficial process. Queensland attempted to use this precedent against the Meriam people in Mabo — the High Court rejected it on human rights grounds.

1921 — The Key Precedent

Amodu Tijani v The Secretary, Southern Nigeria: The most important Privy Council decision for Mabo. Viscount Haldane held that pre-colonial native land rights survived British sovereignty as a "usufructuary right" held communally, qualifying the Crown's "radical title." The Crown's radical title throughout was qualified by these community rights. This framework — radical title in the Crown, native title as a burden upon it — became the direct template for Mabo.

1930 — Chiefly Structure Codified

Sakariyawo Oshodi v Moriamo Dakolo: Viscount Dunedin summarised the Lagos position: the paramount chief owns land not in fee simple but by usufructuary title, with occupiers having rights to remain and transmit holdings. This effectively devolved land control to chiefly authority, enabling indirect rule through legal doctrine.

Radical Title vs Native Title: The Core Distinction

Radical Title

The underlying, sovereign title of the Crown, acquired at the moment of annexation or cession. It does not extinguish pre-existing native rights — it simply sits beneath them.

Native Title

A communal, usufructuary right — a right of use and enjoyment — held by Indigenous communities according to their own laws and customs. It is a "burden" on the Crown's radical title, not an independent proprietary right.

Beneficial Title

The fuller ownership interest. In the Privy Council framework, native title can potentially evolve into individual beneficial ownership as custom evolves — but this evolution is assessed by colonial (settler) courts.

Extinguishment

Native title can be extinguished — permanently and without compensation — by a Crown grant inconsistent with its continuation. In Australia, this became the mechanism for massive loss of Aboriginal land rights.

Silverstein's point: These doctrines were developed to serve the logic of indirect rule in African franchise colonies. Importing them into a settler colony (Australia) — where the object is land acquisition, not labour exploitation — produces particular distortions.

Murray Island: A Colonial Experiment

Silverstein notes a striking historical irony: the Murray Islands, the specific claim area in Mabo, were themselves the site of a late-nineteenth century experiment in indirect rule. In 1878, a British magistrate visited Mer and announced he would support a chief over the Meriam people. When the Murray Islands were annexed by Queensland in 1879, the appointed chief was made magistrate with a small police force.

The system was deemed "tyrannous" and abolished within a few years — but a chiefly figure persisted, running a council into the early twentieth century under Queensland oversight. The legal doctrine recognising Meriam native title thus came full circle: a community briefly governed by an imposed colonial chieftaincy had its traditional connection to land recognised by the same legal system that had sought to govern them through indirect rule.

Reading the Mabo Judgments

The leading judgment was delivered by Justice Brennan (with Deane and Gaudron JJ writing separately in more expansive terms). Silverstein subjects the text of these judgments to critical scrutiny, identifying the ways in which even a decision ostensibly recognising Aboriginal rights perpetuates colonial epistemologies.

Cartography and Discursive Mastery

Silverstein draws attention to how Brennan J begins his judgment: with a precise geographical description of the Murray Islands — their coordinates, dimensions, shape. This is not neutral scene-setting. The invocation of cartography, Silverstein argues, performs a colonial act: it renders the land "entirely knowable," producing it as an empty, measurable space within the national imaginary, effacing Indigenous interpretations of the same territory.

Immediately following, Brennan J turns to an anthropological description of the Meriam people — their communal life, ceremonial practices, gardening, social cohesion. The Meriam are thus "written" as people governed by custom rather than law, lacking individual agency. Silverstein reads this as the continuation of a colonial trope — encapsulating the indigene within anthropological discourse — even within a judgment asserting their rights.

Brennan J thus begins perhaps the most significant judgment purportedly in favour of Aboriginal rights with an exercise in discursive domination over Indigenous people.

— Silverstein (2007), p 73

The "Tide of History"

Perhaps the most telling phrase in Brennan J's judgment is the "tide of history" — the metaphor used to explain how native title may be extinguished when traditional customs have ceased to be observed. The tide washes away native title by impersonal and seemingly natural flow of historical change.

Silverstein reads this as echoing Lord Sumner's language in In re Southern Rhodesia — where colonial dispossession was presented as the "inevitable" replacement of a lower state of things with a higher one. Both formulations mask the violence, physical and epistemic, that accompanied the settler colonial project, specifically by muddying the question of human responsibility.

The practical consequence is that native title carries the seeds of its own destruction: the more colonial contact a community has experienced, the less likely they are to satisfy the continuity requirements. Those most harmed by colonisation are least eligible for the remedy.

The Threshold Test

Claimants must show continuous observance of traditional laws and customs from before sovereignty to the present. The Yorta Yorta people failed this test.

Who is Excluded

Urban Aboriginal communities; those in south-eastern Australia most affected by colonisation; those whose customs changed under colonial pressure — paradoxically, those who suffered most.

The International Law Dimension

The Mabo judgments are striking for their engagement with international and imperial law. The court drew on Privy Council decisions arising from British colonies across the empire (Lagos, Southern Rhodesia, New Zealand) to construct the doctrine of native title for Australia.

Silverstein argues this makes Mabo legible as a domestic encounter with international law in a specific sense: the principles of indirect rule, developed in the context of the League of Nations Mandate System and British imperial administration, were imported wholesale into Australian common law. The decision, read this way, is the culmination of an international legal genealogy rooted in colonial domination.

This has significant implications for how we assess Mabo's "progressive" character. The international law it mobilises was not the international human rights law of the post-war era, but the jurisprudence of empire — crafted to manage colonised peoples rather than liberate them.

It is not only the mechanism of its extinguishment that is embedded in the "recognition" of native title, but also the incorporation of Indigenous ways of knowing and their translation into manageable forms of local governance.

— Silverstein (2007), p 78
